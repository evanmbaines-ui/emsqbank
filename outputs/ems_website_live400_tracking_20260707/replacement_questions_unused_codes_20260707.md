## 351. Hypertensive Pulmonary Edema Treatment Escalation

Question ID: `EMS2026-2-3-2-3-03`
Job ID: `JOB-2-3-2-3-03`
Core content code: `2.3.2.3`
Replaces archived: `EMS2026-2-3-1-4-01` at review order 351

A 68-year-old with hypertension develops abrupt severe dyspnea over 30 minutes. He is sitting upright, diaphoretic, speaking one-word phrases, and has diffuse crackles with scattered wheezes. Oxygen saturation is 82% on a nonrebreather and blood pressure is 224/132 mm Hg. After upright positioning, noninvasive positive-pressure ventilation, and initial sublingual nitroglycerin, he remains severely distressed with blood pressure 204/118 mm Hg. He is awake, tolerating the mask, has no vomiting, and his medication list includes no phosphodiesterase-5 inhibitor. What is the best next treatment priority?

A. Give intravenous furosemide.
B. Treat with nebulized albuterol.
C. Proceed directly to drug-assisted intubation.
D. Escalate nitroglycerin dosing.

Answer: D

Rationale: D is best because this presentation fits hypertensive acute pulmonary edema with severe respiratory distress. When the patient is awake and tolerating noninvasive positive-pressure ventilation, treatment should emphasize positive pressure plus rapid afterload and preload reduction with nitrates as long as blood pressure and contraindication screening permit. A may be used later in selected heart-failure patients but is too slow and should not delay nitrates in this phenotype. B is tempting because wheezing can occur, but the blood pressure, crackles, and abrupt pulmonary edema pattern point away from primary bronchospasm. C may become necessary if airway protection, oxygenation, or mask tolerance fails, but the stem gives conditions favoring continued noninvasive support with vasodilator escalation.

Citation: Cone DC, Brice JH, Delbridge TR, Myers JB, eds. Emergency Medical Services: Clinical Practice and Systems Oversight. 3rd ed. John Wiley & Sons; 2021:56.

## 380. Seizure During Deep Technical Dive

Question ID: `EMS2026-2-3-13-4-5-02`
Job ID: `JOB-2-3-13-4-5-02`
Core content code: `2.3.13.4.5`
Replaces archived: `EMS2026-2-2-6-02` at review order 380

EMS is called to a marina for a 36-year-old technical diver after a wreck dive. His dive partner reports that while still at depth, before any rapid ascent, the diver became anxious, complained of tunnel vision and ringing in his ears, developed lip twitching, and then had a generalized convulsion. After surfacing, he is awake but confused and has no chest pain, dyspnea, or focal neurologic deficit. Which explanation best fits the event the EMS physician should suspect?

A. Nitrogen narcosis.
B. Oxygen toxicity.
C. Arterial gas embolism.
D. Decompression sickness.

Answer: B

Rationale: B is best because central nervous system oxygen toxicity can present unpredictably at depth with visual symptoms, tinnitus, facial or lip twitching, irritability, coordination problems, or convulsions, and seizure may be the first severe manifestation. A is a plausible dive-related distractor, but nitrogen narcosis classically causes impaired judgment rather than the described sensory symptoms, twitching, and convulsion. C is less likely because the convulsion occurred before any rapid ascent and the stem does not describe pulmonary barotrauma or neurologic collapse immediately after surfacing. D can cause neurologic symptoms after surfacing, but it does not best explain the symptom cluster occurring while still at depth.

Citation: Couturier K, Husain I. Diving injury. In: Cone DC, Brice JH, Delbridge TR, Myers JB, eds. Emergency Medical Services: Clinical Practice and Systems Oversight. 3rd ed. John Wiley & Sons; 2021:425.
